What my Credly badges mean (and how anyone can check them)

If you are hiring, collaborating, or simply curious: this page explains my digital badges in plain language, groups them by topic, and links to the official record on Credly so you can verify each one with a single click.

In short

Credly is like a public “certificate wallet” online. Each badge opens a page that shows who issued it and when. Together, these badges show a path from cloud and data fundamentals toward security, governance, and responsible AI—alongside the day-to-day work of building and running systems.

Who might find this useful?

General readers get a clear picture of what each badge represents without needing to know AWS or ISO jargon first. Technical and audit-minded readers can jump to the grouped lists and the table at the end for dates, issuers, and direct assertion links.

What is Credly, in everyday terms?

Many courses and exams now issue a digital badge instead of (or as well as) a PDF. Credly stores those badges on a profile you can share. When someone opens a badge, they see details that are harder to fake than a screenshot: the training company or exam body, the issue date, and sometimes renewal rules.

That public page is often called a verifiable credential or assertion: nothing magical—just a stable link that a third party can use to confirm you really earned the credential.

On my homepage, the Digital badges (Credly) grid reads from a small data file so the badges you see there match what I publish on Credly.

ISO “Lead Auditor” badges — governance in plain words

ISO standards describe how organizations should run certain kinds of management systems—documented processes, roles, risk thinking, and improvement. A Lead Auditor badge means I have been trained to plan and lead audits against those standards: checking evidence, interviewing process owners, and reporting findings in a structured way.

That sits next to engineering work in a useful way: when you design platforms or review architecture, it helps to speak the same language as risk, compliance, and security teams.

  • ISO/IEC 42001:2023 Lead Auditor (26 Apr 2026 · Mastermind Assurance) — focuses on AI management systems (how an organization governs its use of artificial intelligence: roles, risk, lifecycle, and trust). The standard’s abbreviation is often AIMS.
  • ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Lead Auditor (13 Jan 2026 · Mastermind Assurance) — focuses on information security management: keeping data and systems confidential, intact, and available in a provable, process-driven way. You may see this called an ISMS (information security management system).

AWS Academy and AWS Certification — courses vs exam

AWS Academy badges usually mean I completed an official AWS-structured course (often with labs and milestones). They are strong signals of structured learning in topics like architecture, security, data, machine learning, or generative AI.

AWS Certified Cloud Practitioner is different: it is a proctored exam from AWS. It checks broad, foundational knowledge of the AWS Cloud—services, billing ideas, security basics, and how cloud fits into real workloads.

  • AWS Academy — Generative AI Foundations (25 Apr 2026)
  • AWS Academy — Machine Learning Foundations (25 Apr 2026)
  • AWS Academy — Cloud Architecting (21 Feb 2026)
  • AWS Academy — Cloud Security Foundations (20 Dec 2025)
  • AWS Certified Cloud Practitioner (16 Dec 2025)
  • AWS Academy — Data Engineering (5 Mar 2025)

AWS Partner badges — context for readers

These two badges come from AWS Partner training. In practice they mean I studied how AWS talks to customers and partners about value, objections, and technical foundations—useful when cloud adoption is a team sport across sales, solutions, and engineering.

  • AWS Partner — Sales Accreditation (3 Dec 2025)
  • AWS Partner — Technical Accredited (3 Dec 2025)

OPSWAT — critical infrastructure in simple terms

Critical infrastructure means the systems society relies on—energy, water, transport, hospitals, and more. Protecting them blends cybersecurity with physical and operational reality.

The OPSWAT Introduction to Critical Infrastructure Protection (ICIP) badge (10 Jan 2026) introduced me to core ideas in that space and how OPSWAT structures its learning paths—helpful background when thinking about secure operations and safe file transfer in sensitive environments.
